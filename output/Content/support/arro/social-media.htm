Social media made simple

Social media is online media that you can use to market and promote your school and its projects.

Here we’ll take a look at Facebook, LinkedIn, Twitter and Youtube and consider how you can use these to promote your projects.

Within each we include links to managing account security because online security of information is important and if you understand how to make yourself secure online you will be more confident about using it.

It might feel daunting but we recommend starting with one platform and familiarising yourself with that platform before moving to the next. You will soon find it easy to use one or more to share information about your school projects. You may even find it fun and wonder how you managed without these platforms before.

Warning! By its nature, social media is ever-evolving both technologically and in guidelines for use. Social media applications are independent software and are not designed or controlled by Pebble. Therefore the best place to find the most up-to-date instruction and guidance on how to do specific things within individual social media platforms, for example, change your privacy settings within Facebook or secure your social media account, is to look in the help centre for the social media platform. That is what we recommend and we would also advise a regular review of the platform’s processes for security to ensure that you keep your accounts as secure as you can moving forwards.

Facebook

Facebook is a social sharing platform that has grown to encompass business promotion with the use of Facebook pages.

If you use Facebook in your personal life, you will have a personal account on Facebook to connect with friends in real or virtual life and share stories and photos, discuss news and otherwise pass the time of day. If you are an organisation, such as a school, the school will likely have a Facebook account. You have to have an account on Facebook in order to create pages on Facebook. As this guidance is about creating pages for the school projects, we recommend that you use the school Facebook account when setting up school project pages on Facebook. Doing this means that anyone with access to the school Facebook account can post updates to the page and your own personal Facebook account is unaffected.

Why have a Facebook page?

The biggest incentive to creating and using a Facebook page to promote your school project is that you have access to the vast amount of people who use Facebook on a daily basis. However, there is no guarantee that simply having a page will boost interest in your school and its projects. You have to reach out to the people who use Facebook, through posting updates on how the project is progressing, sharing information that can help other people in a similar situation, and inviting people to engage with you and what you are trying to do on your page by having events to raise money or awareness of the project.

The main benefit of having a Facebook page for your school project is to engage with the students. Facebook is one of the ways young people over the age of thirteen begin engaging with social media and if they can see a Facebook page about a school project they are invested in, they can become strong supporters, helping to spread the word by sharing details of the Facebook page for you on their social networks.

Posts on your page

If the page is about your school project, you can create posts which share information about the project, what fund-raising you are doing for the project, what events you have for the project and its fund-raising.

Events

You can create events on your Facebook page to mimic the real-world event. This has the benefit of attracting people to the real-world event if they are in the locality. You can also create events which are virtual, for example, a Q&A event. If you do this, you must make sure to be on hand to provide answers to questions asked during the event times advertised.

Page Likes

The more likes you get on your Facebook page, the better it looks for your popularity and success.

Each time a person clicks Like on the page, this information is shared with their Facebook friends, who may in turn be encouraged to click Like themselves.

Each time you post an update on the project to the Facebook page, this update appears on the newsfeed of everyone who has liked your page. So increased likes gives you a potentially bigger audience for your posts. This is only a potentially bigger audience because there is also a Follow/Unfollow option on a Facebook Page which can affect who sees your page updates.

Follow/Unfollow

When someone clicks Like on your Facebook page, they will automatically follow and see all your Facebook page updates in their your newsfeed.

However, they may decide they don’t want to see all the updates you post and click Unfollow on your Facebook page. Their Like remains in the number of likes your Facebook page has, but your reach is less because they won’t see what you are posting.

Benefits with a fee

Once you have more than 100 likes, you are offered the Boost Post option when you create a post on Facebook. You will be charged a fee for this, depending on the audience you want to reach. However, you don’t have to wait until you get 100 likes. You can create a Facebook Ad for your Facebook page which offers you advanced targeting options you don’t have through Boost Post. Again you’ll need to pay for this.

Making the most of Facebook

After creating a fundraising event for Arro it is a good idea to post it on Facebook so it can reach more people and create interest. You will be able to copy the URL of your Facebook event page and paste it into a post where your audience can see it. You could post about the event multiple times especially if you want to remind people that the event is taking place soon.

You may want to consider
  • The content you write in the Facebook post when sharing your event.

    • Try to make it catchy and interesting but also easy to understand so people joining the event know what they are signing up to.

  • Engaging with the audience.

    • Often people will ask questions about the event by leaving a comment on the post. If you check the post regularly you will be able to answer any questions asked.

  • Monitoring the post for any spam or unwelcome comments.

    • Unfortunately, sometimes people will leave unwelcome comments and you need to remove these as soon as possible to prevent other people being put off by them. Although you can prevent people from posting on your page you cannot disable others comments on your posts. What you can do is hide or delete them and you can also block words and turn on the profanity filter for your Facebook page.

LinkedIn

LinkedIn is a professional social networking platform for individuals and businesses that has grown to encompass thought-leadership within areas of expertise through blogging.

If you use LinkedIn in your personal life, you will have a personal account and profile to advertise your work history and skills to potential future employers. If you are an organisation, such as a school, the school may have a LinkedIn account and a LinkedIn School Page which promotes the school brand and its projects. If not, we recommend that you create a page for the school on LinkedIn, using the school LinkedIn account. Doing this means that anyone with access to the school LinkedIn account can post updates to the page and your own personal LinkedIn account is unaffected.

Why have a LinkedIn School Page?

This is the best way of promoting the school and its motto and projects within LinkedIn. You can post all updates for the school and its projects on the Updates tab. Anyone who is following the page will see the updates you post.

These are the ways of sharing information on your school page:

Share third party content

This is a quick way of sharing content which can help to promote your school and its projects. Find the content you want to share, which could be an interesting article in the local news about your school project, a blog article you have written on LinkedIn’s blogging platform, other school projects, or to a news article about education in general .

Introduce it by sharing your thoughts or reactions to it and then including the URL to the link in your update.

Tip: When you paste the URL wait until metadata has loaded properly and you should see the content description and live link, in other words an image of the article loads and displays and this is what the reader sees rather than the URL.

Upload photographs

This is also a quick way of sharing content that can help promote what you are doing. For example, you could share photos from a fund-raising event, networking event, of your fundraising target, or before and after photos showing the successful outcomes of income generating projects.

Blogging on LinkedIn

You can find articles of interest on LinkedIn’s blogging platform.

You can write articles and publish these on LinkedIn’s blog to share your expertise directly with all of your connections on LinkedIn as well as being visible to people who read the LinkedIn blog. For example, you might have had a successful fund-raising or cost-saving idea which has meant you could achieve your objectives in a smarter way. You can write about this and share it so that others in a similar position can benefit.

If you want to get into this level of sharing it is worth considering:

  • Do you want to be a thought leader? This requires a consistent approach to blogging in content and in expression. It requires a commitment to continually create unique content often.
  • Do you blog elsewhere? Duplicating content on the Internet isn’t recommended so think about the different audiences of where you blog. Your blog posts about projects at the school will be of most interest to the parents and stakeholders in the school and these may be best received on the school blog or community blog. Your articles about the ideas and theories behind why you did something or why it was successful will be most useful to schools in a similar position, so you can adapt the content for the audience, linking from the LinkedIn post to the school blog post and vice versa to gain maximum coverage.
  • How often do you want to write articles? There’s a fine line between saturating the reader and leaving them wanting more. This line falls somewhere between once a week to once a month. More than once a week and you risk alienating readers (as well as exhausting yourself). Less than once a month and you risk losing retention of interest and a sense of continuity for the reader.
Making the most of LinkedIn

LinkedIn can be used in a similar way to Facebook but the audience is different. You engage with business professionals on LinkedIn whereas on Facebook you engage with a students’ parents or members of the local community. You may want to advertise an event on LinkedIn as an opportunity for a business to sponsor your school.

You may want to consider
  • Because the audience is different from that in Facebook, you need to consider adjusting the content of your post on LinkedIn to suit a professional business audience.
  • LinkedIn is not normally a social media channel people use in their leisure time so trying to engage with your school's stakeholder may be challenging.

Twitter

Twitter is a online social network for sharing news.

If you use Twitter in your personal life, you will have a personal account which you will use to share stories in up to 140 characters. It is quite hard at first to convey a message in 140 characters but it becomes easier with practice. You will also have a number of followers and follow other twitter users to read their news. You may also have created different lists of twitter users so that you can easily follow and read different types of news. If you are an organisation, such as a school, the school may have a Twitter account which promotes the school and its projects. If not, we recommend that you create an account for the school on Twitter, and use this account to tweet and share news about the school and its projects. Doing this means that anyone with access to the school Twitter account can post updates to the page and your own personal Twitter account is unaffected, although of course you can retweet and share the school tweets using your personal account.

Why have a school Twitter account?

If you have a school Twitter account, you can grow the network of school students, parents, staff and other stakeholders. Then when you tweet some news about a project, you can expect a certain number of your followers to retweet this news to their networks which will be much larger than your own. There’s no guarantee that simply having a Twitter account and tweeting about your projects will boost interest in your school and its projects but you won’t know if you don’t try. As with all forms of outreach, you have to work to engage with the Twitter followers and retweeters, liking their posts and thanking them for retweeting your news and responding to any questions they might ask you on Twitter.

Following

When you first have Twitter you won’t have any followers. One way of growing your network is to follow others. So grow your network on Twitter by finding and following other schools, educational tweeters, and then begin by tweeting interesting facts about your school or education. Slowly your follower count will go up. You will find that because Twitter suggests people to follow based on who you have followed already you will soon find like-minded individuals and groups to follow. Also your account will be suggested to others in the same way.

Warning!
  • There are plenty of people who will try to sell you followers but these won’t be authentic or have any real value.
  • Before following your new followers, research them by checking out their tweets and their followers and how long they have had an account on Twitter to see if they are authentic. Only follow back if they fit your interest in education or income generation.
Creating lists

When you first join Twitter, you won’t be overwhelmed by the speed with which tweets keep appearing in your newsfeed. But the more people and organisations you follow, the more tweets appear in your newsfeed and you may find it difficult to keep up with what everyone is tweeting. This is why creating lists is a good way to keep up-to-date with different groups of Twitter users. For example, you might want to create a list of educational thought-leaders, so you can quickly see what they are thinking and talking about, or you might want to create a list of people and organisations who support the aims in one of your school projects.

Lists can be public or private so you can create private lists to help you keep up-to-date without letting everybody see the lists. Public lists are good if you want to create a list for others to follow as that saves them time in setting up their own list.

Likes

Liking in Twitter is a quick way of conveying approval and showing interest. It’s also a way people acknowledge when other people mention or thank them in their twitter posts.

Liking is easy to do. You will see Twitter’s Like icon on tweets in your newsfeed. If you want to like something, click the icon and it’s done. Liking is public, so all of the tweets you have liked can be seen from the profile page of your account.

If you want to see how many likes your own tweets have garnered, look at your profile and list of tweets. The number of likes is given below the tweet.

Mentions

This is a tweet that someone posts which mentions you by your Twitter handle (another name for Twitter user name). This is mostly something you want to happen because it brings other people to your account profile and raises awareness of your school account and brand. Mentions can also lead to useful Twitter dialogue between users on subjects of interest. It’s public - all mentioned users and people who follow any of the mentioned users will see the conversation and may join in.

Tip: If you want or need to have a private conversation with another Twitter user, use the Direct Message facility.

Warning! Occasionally spammers may mention random twitter users in tweets that have no relevance and may involve you in areas you don’t want. When anyone mentions your Twitter account in a post, check the post to ensure it is a genuine mention that you are happy to endorse. If not, you can block and report the account so that they won’t do it again.

Retweets

The more your followers retweet your tweets, the greater the potential reach of your message in the Twittersphere and therefore in the real world also. So getting retweets is something you want.

Retweets are easy to do. You will see Twitter’s retweet icon on tweets in your newsfeed. If you want to retweet something to share it with all of your followers, click the icon and it’s done. If you want to see how many retweets your own tweets have garnered, look at your profile and list of tweets. The number of retweets is given below the tweet.

Sometimes users will quote your tweet or put RT at the beginning of their tweet which is another way of sharing your content and message, and crediting you at the same time.

Hashtags

Using hashtags is one of the best ways to spread the word about your project and encourage people outside of your network of followers to visit your project community page.

Hashtags are used to search for and find content on Facebook and Twitter. They allow people to search for their interests and find your posts.

Simply decide on a word that relates to your project and pop a hashtag character (#) in front of it. Examples of hashtags you might use are: #schoolname, #fundraising, #playgrounds, #community and #communityspirit. Try searching on different hashtags on Twitter to see which hashtags might best suit the promotion of your own projects. If you include the hashtag, when anyone searches for it they will see the tweets you have made with the hashtag, even although they aren’t following your Twitter account.

Etiquette

Twitter is a great promotional tool but it is also like an online version of conversational chat. Just as there are rules of etiquette in the real world, so there are in the Twitter Universe. For example,

  • Using proper rather than text-speak.
  • Following back where appropriate.
  • Thanking someone for retweeting your link.
  • Looking to retweet someone’s link in return for their retweeting your link.
  • Crediting someone for their idea.
  • Promoting exchange of information in between promotional tweets.
  • Sticking to conveying all information in one tweet, not spreading it across three or four because you can’t condense the message.
  • Not overusing hashtags.

Note: There are lots of links about etiquette on twitter on the Internet. Here is Mashable’s Complete Guide to Twitter Etiquette.

Making the most of Twitter

The Twitter audience is a mix of professional and casual users. Using hashtags (#) will make your post more visible in more streams. Try to include any trending hashtags that are relevant to your post.

You may want to consider
  • The 140 character limit per Twitter post. You need to keep twitter posts short and sweet.

Youtube

Youtube is a platform for sharing videos. It has, according to Youtube’s statistics, over a billion users. That is a huge potential reach if you want to consider making and sharing video material about your school projects on youtube.

If you use Youtube in your personal life, you will have a personal Google account which you use to sign into Youtube and post videos to share publicly or privately. In addition, when you have signed in, you can subscribe to others’ Youtube channels, create playlists and comment on other people’s videos. Youtube quickly learns what you like to watch and will suggest videos for you to watch in line with what you have already watched.

If you are an organisation, such as a school, the school may have a Google account to sign into Youtube and a Youtube channel which hosts the videos that promote the school and its aims and projects. If not, we recommend that you create a Google account for the school to sign into Youtube and then create a channel to post videos about the school and school projects. You can then tweet a link to the video or share it on Facebook. This will encourage your followers to subscribe to your Youtube channel and be kept up-to-date with new videos that you post. If you set up a school account on Google, then anyone with access to the school Google account can post videos to the channel and your own personal Google account is unaffected.

Why have a Youtube channel?

You can only have a public presence on youtube if you have a youtube channel. Since having a public presence is beneficial for spreading the word about your school and its projects, having a channel is a good thing. But you don’t need to stop at one channel. If you are intending to make and share many videos for a particular project or projects progressing through income generation to construction and delivery, you can create a separate channels for the projects.

This is a good idea if you have or expect to build a different list of subscribers for each channel. For example, if you have primary school projects and senior school projects you might want to build a subscriber list of parents for each channel as they perhaps wouldn’t be as interested in other projects you have.

Warning! If you intend to include videos which feature the pupils, remember you will need to obtain parental or guardian consent. This is a requirement whether the Youtube video is posted as PublicPosting a Youtube video as Public means the video can be seen and shared by anyone., PrivatePosting a Youtube video as Private means that videos can only be seen by you and users that you choose. or UnlistedPosting a Youtube video as Unlisted means it can only be seen and shared by anyone with the URL link to the video..

What’s in a video?

Most people who think of a video think of moving images, recorded live, but you can make a video for Youtube that is more like a moving presentation and based on still images and text. You can combine still and moving images, add special effects such as fade in and out, overlay images with written words, or include voice-over or music as the backdrop to the video.

There are plenty of sources to find images (still and moving) and music which are free to use and licenced with the Creative Commons licence. Often you can use creative works with this licence without attribution but it is always important to include a reference to the Creative Commons licence in your credits and in some cases include a credit to the person who donated the item to the CC licence. You can find out more information about the Creative Commons licence here.

Composing or constructing a video

Before you begin: 

  • Think about the message you want to get across through the video and the audience you are intending the video for.
  • Watch as many videos as you can in the same vein before you start so you get some ideas of how to create yours.
  • Decide on the software you will use to create the video and spend some time playing around with it to familiarise yourself with it and what it can do.

    • Note: There are free options such as Windows Live Movie Maker or Easy Video Maker. You can also do some editing using the Youtube Video Editor. There are also options which you can pay for, such as Adobe Premiere Pro.

  • Draft a potential script, so you know where to begin and where to end.
  • Decide if you want to create voice-over or if you will use words on the screen as text to deliver your message.

    • Note: Beware of voice-over unless you are familiar with recording equipment and the conditions for sound-proofing.

When you are ready to begin, create the movie file in the software you’ve chosen. You should structure each movie to have the following elements:

  • Title - introduces the movie and what it is about, this should be short and allow your Youtube followers to determine whether to watch it or not.
  • Contents - this is the content you have scripted out and can include still images, movies, text on the screen and/or voice-over.
  • Credits - where you list media and credits where applicable, including creative commons attributions.
  • End - signs off from the movie and can include a link to where donations to your project can be made
Making the most of Youtube

If you have created a video to help advertise an event you can share this video across all other social media platforms as long as it isn't bigger than the maximum upload size relevant to each platform. Videos from youtube can also be embedded into your website and/or landing page.

You may want to consider
  • There are certain policies regarding sharing and using certain youtube videos if they are not your own. It would be best practice to research the regulations if you are considering using a video made by another party.
