Why should I share my project on social media?

Tip: You can view screens on this page at full size by clicking on the thumbnail image.

Note: Links on this page lead to external websites and are for guidance on social media use only.

Market research indicates that it takes 6 - 8 contact points to generate a viable lead. This means that you won’t get much success from your campaigns unless you are sharing them - a lot.

The campaigns Pebble provides are designed to take you up to the minimum number of contact points with your stakeholders to encourage them to sign up to help you. However, emailing is only one method of connecting with your customers. In addition you can print flyers for your projects and hand these out to people face-to-face.

But there’s one thing you can do above everything which will give you far greater reach than talking or emails alone. That is sharing news about your project on social media.

Email works very well because creating and maintaining mailing lists is easy and there are good guidelines for building these lists and data protection. The school is in control of the message they want to send out and indeed of initiating it being sent, and if your stakeholders don’t like receiving emails they can easily unsubscribe. Generally, emails that are sent to subscribed users are for those users only. Sharing news about your projects and their successes on social media is opening up the news to a world audience. This adds huge potential value to your email campaigns by pushing your message out into a broader network of people and organisations. By gathering likes on your tweets and shares, click-throughs to your projects and interactions, you push awareness of the project in the wider world.

There are a great many articles on the Internet about social reach and what it can mean for you. Click here to read a Business 2 Community article about social reach written in 2014.

What methods of sharing can you use?

  Method Setup tasks Ongoing tasks Can you measure the success
1. Verbal Create a list of who you speak to. Tell people you meet and ask them to spread the word. You can if you ask them what prompted them to contact you and keep a record of this.
2. Email Create email lists of stakeholders. Send emails to your stakeholders. Depending on your email software, you can perhaps receive Read Receipt notifications that tell you the stakeholder has opened the email.
3. Social media Create a Facebook page or Twitter account. Share project successes on Twitter and Facebook.

If you have shared to your social media accounts, you will see the results in Likes and Retweets and Shares. All of these reach a much wider population than verbal and email methods of communication alone.

Whatever the method of sharing you choose, you have to do some setup tasks and then some ongoing tasks. However, as you move from the real to the virtual share, the capability of sharing more frequently and reaching more people than before increases exponentially. Why? Social media users have short attention spans. Studies have shown that Facebook posts reach their peak engagement 90 minutes after being posted and Twitter after only 18 minutes! In other words, you need to share and keep on sharing to keep your project in the forefront of their minds.

Social media strategies

With some strategic planning you can make the most of your social media sharing opportunities. You can pin posts so these are the first thing your readers see when they come to your Facebook page or Twitter feed. You can use hashtags to attract an even wider audience. And you can schedule tweets to capture the maximum number of readers.

Pinning posts

Once you begin using Twitter and Facebook and grow in confidence, you’ll find numerous tricks to help you extend your reach even further. For example, you can pin your posts in Facebook to the top of the page, or pin the tweet at the top of your Twitter feed. This guarantees your project is the first post people will see when they come to your page or Twitter feed.:

Using hashtags

Using hashtags is the best way to spread the word about your project and encourage people outside of your network of followers to visit your project community page.

Hashtags are used to search for and find content on Facebook and Twitter. They allow people to search for their interests and find your posts.

Simply decide on a word that relates to your project and pop a hashtag character (#) in front of it. Examples of hashtags you might use are: #<<schoolname, #fundraising, #playgrounds, #community and #communityspirit.

How often should you share?

Social media users fall into different patterns of reading behaviour - there are people who read posts in the morning, those who check in during the afternoon and those who only log on in the evening and so on.

The effectiveness of your strategy depends not only on this but also on the social platform you are using. For example, the most effective way of driving traffic to your project community page is by posting about the project weekly. However, Twitter feeds fly off the screen quickly and with this in mind, it’s not unreasonable to tweet three times during a day about your projects, beginning with appropriate text e.g. “For the morning readers ....”, “For the afternoon audience ...”,  “For those logging on this evening ...” However, in order to make your Twitter feed appealing, rather than simply have a long list of tweets all about your project, intersperse these tweets with tweets about interesting things around the project.
