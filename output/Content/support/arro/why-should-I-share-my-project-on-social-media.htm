Why should I share my project on social media?

Market research indicates that it takes 6 - 8 contact points to generate a viable lead. This means that you won’t get much success from your campaigns unless you are sharing them - a lot.

The campaigns Pebble provides are designed to take you up to the minimum number of contact points with your stakeholders to encourage them to sign up to help you. However, emailing is only one method of connecting with your customers. In addition you can print flyers for your projects and hand these out to people face-to-face.

But there’s one thing you can do above everything which will give you far greater reach than talking or emails alone. That is social media sharing.

We understand that this can be a scary idea if you have never signed up to any social media tools in the past. You probably hear about how it all goes wrong for people who tweet things and then are sued for what they have tweeted or trolled for their beliefs. This happens because people don’t take care what they tweet or share - either they tweet or share inaccurate information about a person or organisation, or tweet a personal point of view which may be inappropriate for public consumption. Social media sharing is easiest to get right when you are sharing facts and this is exactly what you will be doing when you share information about your projects.

Your school project is x. It’s purpose is y. The benefits are z. You need £££ to fund it. You have x contributors. You need y more. You can sign up here.

 

Because the audience is the public - that is everyone in the world who follows your Facebook page or Twitter account - it can be particularly daunting to start using. So let’s take a look in more detail why you should be doing it.

Email works very well in a twentieth century marketing viewpoint. Creating and maintaining mailing lists is easy and there are good guidelines for building these lists and data protection. The school is in control of the message they want to send out and indeed of initiating it being sent, and if your stakeholders don’t like receiving emails they can easily unsubscribe. Generally, emails that are sent to subscribed users are for those users only. Forwarding them on isn’t appropriate.

Social media, which is coming into its own in the twenty first century, adds huge potential value to your email campaigns by pushing your message out into a broader network of people and organisations. By gathering likes on your tweets and shares, click-throughs to your projects and interactions, you push awareness of the project in the wider world and social media as a marketing channel is growing. There is little cost to the business other than the time to compose a message or a number of messages in 140 characters and schedule the broadcast of these messages across time to ensure you capture attention for morning, afternoon, evening and night-time users of social media.

We know most people find it difficult or scary to think of using social media, especially if you haven’t done so before. There are lots of pitfalls and some very public

aThere are different methods of sharing information and getting your message out in the wider world, but why is it so important to share your project on social media? Quite simply the reach and returns are phenomenal compared to the other methods.

 

What are the methods of sharing you can use?

Verbal - tell people you meet

Email - send emails to your stakeholders

Share on social media - tweet and share facebook posts about your project
